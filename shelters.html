Shelters & Housing Rights

Understanding your legal rights to housing is crucial when facing homelessness. This guide focuses on the important 56-day rule and other housing rights you can rely on.

Need Immediate Help?

If you're facing homelessness tonight or within the next 7 days, call your local council's housing department immediately. Outside office hours, many councils have an emergency phone number.

National Emergency Helpline: 0808 800 4444

You can also call our support team: 0800 123 4567 (24/7)

The 56-Day Rule Explained

What is the 56-Day Rule?

Under the Homelessness Reduction Act 2017, local councils have a legal duty to help prevent your homelessness if you are threatened with homelessness within 56 days (8 weeks). This is known as the "Prevention Duty."

Important: This is twice as long as the previous 28-day rule, giving you more time to get help before you actually become homeless.

If you're already homeless, councils have a "Relief Duty" to help you secure accommodation for at least 6 months.

When Does the 56-Day Clock Start?

The 56-day period starts when either:

  • You notify the council that you're at risk of homelessness within 56 days
  • You receive a valid Section 21 notice from your landlord (the "no-fault" eviction notice)
  • Your fixed-term tenancy is ending within 56 days
  • You're asked to leave family/friends accommodation within 56 days
  • You're at risk of losing your home for any other reason within 56 days

Remember: The earlier you approach the council, the better chance they have to help prevent your homelessness.

Timeline of the 56-Day Rule

Day 1
Threatened with homelessness - You receive eviction notice, are asked to leave accommodation, or realize you'll be homeless within 56 days. Contact your council immediately!
Day 1-5
Initial assessment - Council must assess your needs and provide a Personalized Housing Plan (PHP)
Day 1-56
Prevention Duty period - Council must take "reasonable steps" to help prevent your homelessness
Day 56+
If homelessness not prevented - Prevention Duty ends, Relief Duty begins if you become homeless
56-112
Relief Duty period - Council must take reasonable steps to help you secure accommodation for at least 6 months
Day 112+
Main Housing Duty assessment - If still homeless, council assesses if they owe you the main housing duty

What Support Should You Receive During the 56 Days?

During the 56-day prevention period, the council should:

  • Assess your housing and support needs
  • Create a Personalized Housing Plan with specific actions
  • Take "reasonable steps" to help prevent your homelessness
  • Provide advice and assistance
  • Consider whether temporary accommodation is needed
  • Work with you to explore all housing options

Common prevention measures include:

  • Mediation with landlords or family members
  • Financial assistance to clear rent arrears
  • Help with a rent deposit for new accommodation
  • Negotiating with landlords to extend tenancies
  • Referrals to supported housing options
  • Help applying for benefits or discretionary housing payments

Know Your Housing Rights

Your Legal Rights Under the Homelessness Reduction Act

The Homelessness Reduction Act 2017 strengthened housing rights for those facing homelessness. Here are the key rights you should know:

  1. Right to prevention assistance - If you're at risk of homelessness within 56 days
  2. Right to relief assistance - If you're already homeless
  3. Right to a written assessment - Covering your circumstances and needs
  4. Right to a Personalized Housing Plan - With specific steps for you and the council
  5. Right to information - About your housing options and available support
  6. Right to emergency accommodation - If you're in priority need and homeless
  7. Right to review decisions - If you disagree with the council's assessments
  8. Right to access your case records - To see what information the council holds

Remember: These rights apply regardless of whether you're considered to be in "priority need" or "intentionally homeless" during the prevention and relief stages.

Priority Need Categories

While the 56-day rule applies to everyone, those in "priority need" have additional rights, particularly for emergency accommodation. You may be in priority need if:

  • You have dependent children living with you
  • You're pregnant
  • You're homeless due to domestic abuse
  • You're vulnerable due to old age, mental illness, disability, or other special reason
  • You're aged 16-17 (or 18-20 if previously in care)
  • You're homeless due to an emergency such as flood or fire
  • You're vulnerable as a result of leaving the armed forces, prison, or care

What "vulnerable" means: The legal test is whether you would be "significantly more vulnerable than an ordinary person would be if they became homeless." This is assessed on a case-by-case basis.

Even if you don't fall into these categories, the council still has duties to help under the 56-day rule.

Intentional Homelessness

Councils may assess whether you became homeless "intentionally." This means you deliberately did or failed to do something that led to your homelessness.

Important: Even if the council believes you're intentionally homeless, they still must help you under the 56-day Prevention and Relief Duties.

Examples that are usually NOT considered intentional:

  • Leaving accommodation due to domestic abuse
  • Being unable to afford rent due to a genuine financial crisis
  • Being evicted because your landlord wants to sell or move back in
  • Living in poor conditions that affected your health
  • Losing your home after a relationship breakdown

If the council decides you're intentionally homeless, you can:

  • Ask for a review of this decision within 21 days
  • Get help from a housing advisor to challenge the decision
  • Provide additional evidence about your circumstances
  • Still receive 56 days of assistance under Prevention/Relief duties

Emergency Shelter Options

Immediate Options When Facing Homelessness Tonight

If you might be homeless tonight or very soon:

  1. Contact your local council's housing department immediately - Even outside office hours, many councils have emergency numbers
  2. Call Shelter's emergency helpline: 0808 800 4444 - Open 8am-8pm weekdays, 9am-5pm weekends
  3. Use the StreetLink app or website - If you're sleeping rough or see someone who is
  4. Check local night shelters - Many operate on a first-come, first-served basis
  5. Call our 24/7 helpline: 0800 123 4567 - We can advise on local emergency options

Temporary Accommodation Through the Council

If you're eligible, in priority need, and not intentionally homeless, the council must provide you with temporary accommodation while they investigate your case.

This accommodation might be:

  • A hostel or B&B (usually only for a short period)
  • Temporary supported housing
  • A private rented flat or house
  • Accommodation owned by the council or housing association

Important: If you have children or are pregnant, the council should not place you in bed and breakfast accommodation for more than 6 weeks.

You may have to pay for temporary accommodation, but you might qualify for Housing Benefit or Universal Credit to help with costs.

Night Shelters and Hostels

Night shelters and hostels can provide immediate emergency accommodation, even if you're not eligible for council help:

  • Night Shelters - Basic overnight accommodation, often free, sometimes with a small charge
  • Hostels - More permanent accommodation with support services
  • Winter Shelters - Additional emergency accommodation during cold weather (usually November to March)

Our helpline (0800 123 4567) can provide up-to-date information on availability in your area.

Council Legal Obligations

What Councils MUST Do Under the Law

Local councils have specific legal obligations under the Housing Act 1996 (as amended by the Homelessness Reduction Act 2017):

Initial Assessment

The council must assess if you're homeless or threatened with homelessness, and determine your support needs.

Personalized Housing Plan

The council must work with you to develop a written plan outlining steps both you and the council will take.

Prevention Duty (56 days)

The council must take "reasonable steps" to prevent your homelessness if you're at risk within 56 days.

Relief Duty (56 days)

If you become homeless, the council must take reasonable steps to help you secure accommodation for at least 6 months.

Main Housing Duty

If you're still homeless after the relief duty ends, the council must assess if you're in priority need and not intentionally homeless.

Emergency Accommodation

If there's reason to believe you're in priority need, the council must provide emergency accommodation while investigating.

When Councils Are Failing Their Legal Duties

Signs that a council may not be fulfilling their legal obligations:

  • Refusing to take a homelessness application
  • Not providing a written decision
  • Failing to provide emergency accommodation when there's reason to believe you're in priority need
  • Not providing a Personalized Housing Plan
  • Taking too long to make decisions (more than 15 working days)
  • Requiring unnecessary documentation before helping
  • Sending you away without proper assessment when you approach for help
  • Claiming they have "no duty" to someone clearly threatened with homelessness within 56 days
  • Not acknowledging the 56-day prevention period

If this happens to you:

  1. Ask to speak to a housing manager
  2. Request all decisions in writing
  3. Contact Shelter (0808 800 4444) or a local law center for advocacy
  4. Use our template letters to challenge decisions
  5. Formally complain using the council's complaints procedure

How to Challenge Council Decisions

If you disagree with a council's decision about your homelessness application:

  1. Request a review within 21 days - This must be done in writing (email or letter)
  2. Get advice - Contact Shelter, a law center, or Citizens Advice
  3. Submit additional evidence - About your circumstances or vulnerabilities
  4. Request temporary accommodation - While the review is being conducted
  5. Consider further appeal - To the County Court on a point of law if the review is unsuccessful

The council must complete the review within 8 weeks (or longer by agreement) and give their decision in writing with reasons.

Evidence Checklist

When approaching the council for help, try to bring as much of the following documentation as possible:

  • ID documents (passport, driving license, birth certificate)
  • Immigration status documents (if applicable)
  • Eviction notice or section 21 notice (if you've received one)
  • Tenancy agreement
  • Letters from landlord or family/friends asking you to leave
  • Medical evidence (doctor's letters about health conditions)
  • Evidence of income (payslips, benefit letters)
  • Information about your current housing situation
  • Details of any support workers or social workers
  • Evidence of domestic abuse (if applicable and safe to provide)

Remember: If you don't have all these documents, the council should still help you. They can't refuse to take a homelessness application because you don't have certain documents.
